# Running a Homestead — Getting Started Guide

A practical, no-nonsense guide to starting and running a small homestead. Whether you have a quarter acre or forty, the principles are the same: grow food, raise animals, preserve the harvest, and build systems that sustain your family with less dependence on the grocery supply chain.

## Land Assessment and Planning

Before you plant a single seed or buy your first chicken, assess what you have. Walk your property with a notebook and answer these questions:

**Sun exposure**: Which areas get 6+ hours of direct sunlight? That's where your garden goes. Fruit trees need full sun. Shade-tolerant crops (lettuce, spinach, herbs) can go in partial shade areas.

**Water access**: Where are your spigots? Where does rainwater naturally flow? Gravity is free — use it. Plan your garden and animal areas downhill from water sources where possible. A rainwater harvesting system (gutters → barrel → drip irrigation) is one of the highest-ROI first projects on any homestead.

**Soil quality**: Get a soil test from your county extension office (~$15-25). It tells you pH, macronutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium), and organic matter percentage. Most garden vegetables want pH 6.0-7.0. You can amend anything, but knowing your starting point saves years of guessing.

**Microclimates**: Every property has them. South-facing slopes warm faster in spring. North sides stay cooler longer. Walls and fences create thermal mass. Windbreaks protect tender plants. Map these and use them.

**Zoning and regulations**: Check your county/city regulations for livestock, structures, fencing, and water rights before you invest. Many suburban areas allow chickens but not roosters. Some allow goats under a certain count. Know the rules before you build.

## The Garden — Your First Priority

The garden produces more calories per square foot per dollar invested than any other homestead system. Start here.

### Raised Beds vs In-Ground

**Raised beds** (12-18 inches high, 4 feet wide, any length):
- Better drainage, faster soil warming in spring
- Less bending (matters more than you think at year 3+)
- You control the soil mix completely
- Cost: $50-200 per 4x8 bed depending on materials
- Best for: clay soil, poor drainage, small spaces, beginners

**In-ground** (amended native soil):
- Lower startup cost
- Unlimited scale
- Deeper root zone for perennials and root crops
- Requires more initial soil amendment work
- Best for: good native soil, larger gardens, established homesteads

### What to Grow First

Focus on crops that are expensive to buy, easy to grow, and high-yielding:

1. **Tomatoes** — one plant produces 10-15 lbs per season. A $3 seedling replaces $40+ of grocery tomatoes.
2. **Herbs** (basil, cilantro, rosemary, thyme, oregano, mint) — fresh herbs cost $2-4 per tiny package. One plant produces all season.
3. **Lettuce and greens** (lettuce, spinach, kale, Swiss chard) — cut-and-come-again varieties produce for months. Plant every 2 weeks for continuous harvest.
4. **Peppers** — hot and sweet, prolific producers, excellent for preservation (drying, pickling, freezing).
5. **Zucchini / summer squash** — one plant produces more than a family can eat. The classic "leaving zucchini on your neighbor's porch" crop.
6. **Green beans** — bush beans produce in 50-60 days, pole beans produce for months. Easy to can or freeze.
7. **Cucumbers** — fast growing, high yield, easy to pickle.
8. **Winter squash / pumpkins** — store for 3-6 months without any preservation. Plant in May, eat through December.

### Composting

Every homestead needs a compost system. It's free fertilizer made from your own waste stream.

**The basics:**
- **Browns** (carbon): dried leaves, straw, cardboard, wood chips, sawdust
- **Greens** (nitrogen): kitchen scraps, fresh grass clippings, coffee grounds, manure
- **Ratio**: roughly 3:1 browns to greens by volume
- **Turn it** every 1-2 weeks to aerate (or use a tumbler)
- **Finished compost** in 2-6 months depending on method and climate

**Hot composting** (actively managed, turned frequently) kills weed seeds and pathogens. **Cold composting** (pile it and wait) takes longer but requires less effort. Both produce usable compost.

## Chickens — The Gateway Livestock

Chickens are the most forgiving first animal for new homesteaders. They produce eggs daily, eat kitchen scraps and garden pests, and generate manure for your compost pile.

### Getting Started

- **Start with 4-6 hens** (no rooster needed for eggs). Check local regulations for maximum flock size.
- **Breeds for beginners**: Rhode Island Red (reliable layers, cold-hardy), Buff Orpington (gentle, good layers, broody), Easter Eggers (colorful eggs, friendly), Barred Plymouth Rock (dual-purpose, calm).
- **Coop requirements**: 4 sq ft per bird inside, 10 sq ft per bird in the run. Ventilation is more important than insulation. Predator-proof construction is non-negotiable — hardware cloth (not chicken wire), secure latches, buried apron around the base.
- **Nesting boxes**: 1 box per 3-4 hens, 12x12x12 inches, filled with straw or pine shavings.
- **Roosting bars**: 8-10 inches per bird, 2-4 feet off the ground, higher than the nesting boxes (chickens always roost at the highest available point).

### Daily Care

- **Morning**: open coop, check water (clean and refill if needed), check feed (layer pellets free-choice), collect eggs.
- **Evening**: close coop (predator protection), quick head count.
- **Weekly**: clean coop bedding (deep litter method is least labor), check for mites/lice, observe flock behavior for illness.
- **Monthly**: deep clean if using non-deep-litter method, check coop structure for wear.

### Egg Production

- Most breeds lay 250-300 eggs per year per hen in their first two years.
- Production declines ~20% per year after year 2.
- Hens need 14-16 hours of light to lay consistently. In winter, supplement with a light on a timer (dawn, not dusk — you don't want them roosting in the dark).
- Eggs are shelf-stable unwashed for 2-3 weeks on the counter (the bloom protects them). Washed eggs must be refrigerated.

## Food Preservation

Growing food is half the equation. Preserving it is the other half. The four primary methods:

### Canning (Water Bath and Pressure)

**Water bath canning** is for high-acid foods: fruits, jams, jellies, pickles, tomatoes (with added acid). Equipment: large pot with rack, mason jars, lids, rings. Cost: $30-50 to start.

**Pressure canning** is for low-acid foods: vegetables, meats, soups, beans. Requires a pressure canner (not a pressure cooker). Equipment: $80-150 for the canner plus jars. Essential for safe long-term preservation of non-acidic foods.

### Dehydrating

Remove moisture to prevent bacterial growth. Works for fruits, vegetables, herbs, jerky. A basic dehydrator costs $40-80. Dehydrated food stores for 6-12 months in airtight containers.

### Freezing

The simplest preservation method. Blanch vegetables before freezing (stops enzyme activity). Vacuum sealing extends freezer life from 6 months to 2+ years. Deep freezers (chest style) are more efficient than upright.

### Fermentation

Lacto-fermentation preserves vegetables (sauerkraut, kimchi, pickles) using salt and naturally occurring lactobacillus bacteria. No special equipment needed — salt, vegetables, a jar, and time. Fermented foods also provide probiotics for gut health.

## Permaculture Principles on the Homestead

Permaculture is design science applied to land use. The core idea: work with natural systems instead of against them.

**Key principles for homesteaders:**

1. **Observe before you act.** Spend a full year watching your land before making permanent changes. Where does water flow? Where does frost settle? What grows naturally?
2. **Stack functions.** Every element should serve multiple purposes. Chickens: eggs + pest control + compost + entertainment. Fruit trees: food + shade + windbreak + pollinator habitat.
3. **Use edges.** The boundary between two ecosystems (forest edge, pond edge, garden-to-lawn transition) is the most productive zone. Maximize edge in your design.
4. **Catch and store energy.** Rainwater, sunlight, wind, thermal mass. Every input you can capture on-site is an input you don't buy.
5. **Produce no waste.** Kitchen scraps → compost → garden soil → food. Chicken manure → compost → garden. Every output is an input for something else.

## Water Management

Water is the single most valuable resource on a homestead. Manage it actively.

**Rainwater harvesting**: A 1,000 sq ft roof in an area that gets 30 inches of rain per year captures roughly 18,000 gallons annually. That's enough to irrigate a substantial garden through dry spells. Storage: IBC totes ($50-100 used), 55-gallon drums ($15-30), or purpose-built cisterns for larger scale.

**Drip irrigation**: Uses 30-50% less water than overhead sprinklers. Delivers water directly to root zones. Reduces leaf wetness (fewer fungal diseases). A basic drip kit for a 4x8 raised bed costs $15-25.

**Swales and berms**: Earthworks that capture rainwater runoff and allow it to infiltrate slowly. Swales are level ditches on contour; berms are the mounded soil from digging the swale. Together they recharge groundwater and create productive planting zones on the berm.

## First-Year Timeline

**Month 1-2**: Soil test, garden plan, order seeds, build/prepare beds, set up compost.
**Month 3-4**: Plant cool-season crops (lettuce, peas, spinach, radishes). Start warm-season seedlings indoors.
**Month 4-5**: Transplant warm-season crops after last frost. Build chicken coop.
**Month 5-6**: Get chicks (or started pullets if you want eggs sooner). First harvests begin.
**Month 6-8**: Peak harvest season. Start preserving — can tomatoes, freeze beans, dry herbs.
**Month 8-10**: Plant fall garden (second round of cool-season crops). Clean up spent summer plantings. Add to compost.
**Month 10-12**: Plan next year based on what worked and what didn't. Order seed catalogs. Rest.

## The Honest Truth About Homesteading

It will not save you money in year one. Probably not in year two either. The startup costs (beds, soil, tools, coop, fencing, irrigation) add up. What it DOES do:

- Gives you food security that no grocery store can match
- Produces food that tastes better than anything you can buy
- Teaches skills that transfer across every domain (problem-solving, biology, weather reading, animal husbandry, construction, preservation chemistry)
- Connects your family to the land and to each other
- Builds resilience — the homesteader who already grows food is the person who doesn't panic when the supply chain hiccups

The best time to start a homestead was five years ago. The second best time is this spring. Start small, learn as you go, and build on what works.
